I spent some time looking at the listing the way a buyer's agent shopping the 78130 starter market would have looked at it. A few things stood out, and they are all things that move on a relist. Below is what I would change. No pitch, just an honest second opinion.

1The asking price was sitting above where buyers were willing to engage
$310,000 lands roughly 6 to 11 percent above the top of the valuation range a buyer's agent would have pulled before showing the home. In a builder-tract neighborhood where several near-identical 3-bed floor plans are on the market at any given time, buyers tend to filter on price first and narrative second. That is the most common reason a clean, recent-build home goes 28 days with no offer worth taking. A relist priced inside the value range, with one strong reason to choose this house over the next one on the block, usually moves quickly.
2There is a square footage discrepancy across the listing sites
The MLS feed shows 1,651 square feet. Realtor.com, Movoto, and a few other syndication sites show 1,459. That is a 13 percent difference between sources, and buyers and their lenders see it the moment they pull comps. It does not kill a deal on its own, but it pushes offers down and creates lender-side friction once an offer comes in. Resolving this in writing before a relist - one authoritative number, sourced and disclosed - removes the friction entirely.
3You are competing directly with new-build inventory at the same price
The home is a 2023 build, which is a real strength. The challenge is that the builders along this corridor are still moving fresh inventory in the same price band, often with rate buy-downs and closing-cost incentives. To a price- sensitive first-time buyer, "lightly used 2023" has to do more work to win against "brand new with builder incentives." A relist that leads with what the home has that the new builds do not - a finished yard, blinds and curtain rods already up, no construction next door for the next two years - changes the math for that buyer.
428 days is short - this expired before it had a story
Most listings in this segment do not even start to tell the full marketing story until day 30 or 45. A short DOM expired is almost always a signal that the listing agreement ran out before the listing got the one repositioning that would have moved it. The good news is, you have not absorbed any DOM damage yet. A clean relist with a fresh MLS number, a tighter price, and the sqft issue resolved tends to perform very differently than the first run did.
